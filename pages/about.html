About quickler

quickler exists because the useful work usually happens before the admin catches up.

That started with workflow systems that cut repeated admin for real organisations, then sharpened into two clearer directions: site reports for evidence-heavy field work, and AI agents for broader workflows that need context, memory, and structure.

quickler story

Built around one repeated frustration

The repeated pattern was obvious long before the company name existed: the facts were already there, but someone still had to rebuild the final report, document, approval trail, or decision afterwards. quickler is built to reduce that second pass.

Founder

Philip Ross

Philip Ross studied Electrical and Mechanical Engineering at the University of Strathclyde and later worked at Dijuno, an AI analytics startup, building experience in LLM testing, prompt engineering, automation, and workflow design. That background is what pushed quickler toward serious systems with clear operating value, not vague tech theatre.

Delivered proof

The origin story already has real delivery behind it

Before quickler became a public company, Philip had already delivered six workflow systems across charities, publishing, and commercial operations. Those builds measured 51.5 working days per year saved in the portfolio, with an average return of roughly 12x on time invested.

Private build

The same thinking is used on founder-built AI systems too

Philip also built a private AI cycling coach that loads ride data, calendar context, training rules, and recent history before answering. It is a useful example of the kind of context-aware agent work quickler can build without treating AI as a blank chatbot.

Now

Site Reports and AI Agents

quickler now presents two clearer paths. Site Reports is for evidence-heavy jobs that still get written up later. AI Agents is for broader workflows where the system needs to load context, follow rules, and produce useful work without losing human oversight.
